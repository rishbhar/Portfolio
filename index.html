  Academic Design Project

Assistive Technology for Functional Illiterates


Role

Research, Ideation, Sketching, Usability Testing

Team

Annarose Girvin, Sarah Kiner, Prashanth Chandrasekar, Rishabh Bhardwaj

Duration

3.5 weeks

This project aimed to target a problem for a specific group of people, the Unexotic Underclass. Not a lot of many entrepreneurs and innovators are desgining products for these groups of people. Their problems usually go unnoticed, or they don't have access to the very best which is available in the market. So, for us this project was a pathway to understanding how we design a solution to a specific problem for one of these groups. We were asked to decide on a group and the problem we wanted to design for, which made the whole process more challenging and intriguing!
Problem

We can end up in a very difficult situation if we are not able to follow our medical presriptions correctly. In some cases, not following the prescription correctly could even lead to hospitalization. Or it can have some dangerous side effects! For functional illiterates, (those that speak English, but struggle with reading and writing) this was one of the big problems that they faced in their daily lives. The inability just doesn't affect them but could also comprosise the health of their family members.



Research

It is no denying that the medical information landscape is exhausting. The information on the packages, bottles and prescriptions can be overwhelming. Besides the medicine intake, these packages and medicine bottles have a lot of information on side effects and warnings too. Will all this information, we are getting to know a little about what could cause problems for a functional illiterate. Also, we wanted to know more so we started doing secondary research by reading about the problems faced by the adult illiterates in this area. We also started looking up already available solutions for the problem.



We also met with Bethany Teryy, Coordinator for the Literacy program, VITAL at the Monroe County Public Library in Bloomington. From her we got to know that they used a lot many iPad and mobile applications for teaching the functional illiterates.

We were also able to squash a few our assumptions using user journey maps. To gather information for the journey map, we met with a few pharmacists too.




After we got to know from our research that a lot many mobile apps are available for medical prescriptions, we wanted to know what problem were they targeting. We found out that they were more focussed towards being reminders apps. Mostly all of them were text heavy, with minimum visual aids.





From our research we found out that the current medical prescription landscape. We chose to design to make the medical prescriptions and lables more explicit to functional illiterate adults.



Insights

From our primary and secondary research we found out:

These people were embarrased about their problem.
It affected them more for the accute illnesses.
Audio and visual aids are very helpful for learning new things.
They could understand hand drawn simple icons more easily.
They wanted to do this independently without much help from others.


And through our insights, came our design core

Making medical prescriptions and labels explicit to functional illiterate adults.

Design Process

There were a few thing that we did in this project which stood out, and we realized that it was worth reflecting back and learning form them.


EXPANSION & CONTRACTION

As this project didn't has it all laid down about the user group and their problem, we had to explore different user groups and their problems.







While doing this exercise of expansion and contraction, we continued doing extensive research. It helped us decide on our user group and the problem we wanted to solve for them.


DECIDER PROTOCOLS

The problem landscape grew with expansion. There were multiple problems for each user group. So using the decider protocols for this project really helped us narrow down. At times, it also enabled us to express our opinions when we thought differently.


ROUND ROBIN SKETCHING

We did round robin sketching, and did a lot of it! It helped us build of off each other's ideas, and finally unify them all. It helped us understand each other perspective of what they thought solutions could be like, and that is a great way to learn from each other too!






What I learned

Clearly establishing what the design is ,and what it is not stabalizes decision making.
Using journey maps for an unknown user group and their problem helps squash assumptions.

  Academic Design Project

UBER Ratings Redesign


Role

User Research/Survey, User Personas, Sketching, Usability Testing

Team

Shiyue Sun, Evan Russel, Rishabh Bhardwaj

Duration

2 weeks

Constraints provide us with the scope of our design. They bound us in limits, but that is where we start to push it and explore creative ideas. UBER ratings redesign project was about embracing constraints and understanding their importance in the design process. For the project, we knew who our users were but we had to decide what problem we would be solving for them. We had to come up with a DESIGN CORE and a MANTRA! We tried to make sure that our design was the destination to which our CORE guided us to, and that our MANTRA resonated through our design.
Problem

We were asked to design a more compelling rating system for UBER. It could have been a complete overhaul of their system, or a small variation. We also had to keep in mind that the final design was going to be a part of a larger system (the UBER app).



Research

We had to understand what really the problem was, and to begin, we asked questions. We began with the first phase of our process to understand the current UBER system, and tried it figure out why it was like it was. We brainstormed about UBER's rating system, and came up with questions like:

Why do riders rate drivers and vice versa?
Why only numbers for ratings? Why not something else?
Why is there only one parameter for rating the whole experience?
Is the system biased towards riders?
How does the rating system affect a short term and a long term driver?
Is there something for expectionally good/bad behaviour by a driver?
How motivated are the riders to rate their ride? and How often do they rate their ride?
Do riders understand how the system affects drivers? Do they need to?

and many more... While we were exploring, we also started sketching.


Insights

We continued our secondary research to get more information on the subject. We found out that there has been a lot of agitation among the drivers for UBER's rating system. We interviewed two drivers in Bloomington and got to know about their opinion on the rating system. All this research told us a lot about the drivers and how they were trying to cope up with the consequences of the rating system. But we knew little about the big population of riders, so to get an idea we built a survey form Uber Passengers Survey , with a few questions and shared it with people on social media, twitter etc.

If a driver's average rating is anything below 4.6, he is in the danger zone.
Experienced drivers knew that even a single rating below 4.5 from a rider can affect their employment with UBER.
Most of the riders had no idea that anything below a 5 could drag down a driver's rating into that danger zone.

These findings were surprising! It made us realize how things, that we take for granted, could be of such consquences. And through our insights, came our design core

Design Process

We were introduced to the concept of LENSES in the project prompt. "Lenses, taken in order, provide a kind of brainstorming activity. Each lens builds on the output of the previous one. This framework is designed to iteratively develop a tangible and actionable output of methodologies and best practices.” (from the prompt)





Lenses which helped us the most were.


UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM

The first lens is about reframing the problem into an answerable questions. We started coming up with questions about everything that we didn't understand in the prompt. We compiled all the questions that we found compelling and stepped into the research phase.






SKECTHING & BRAINSTORMING

We learned that sketches sparked conversation. We came up more ideas and questions when we started sketching out our ideas. We sketched as many ideas we could by following the YES, AND approach.







NEGATING & STRENGTHENING

At this stage, we had a design that we could critique among ourselves. We started to think out the situations/reasons our design might not work! and obviously there were some.

Users might not want to rate a driver as their favorite.
A rider might think that he cannot clearly communicate the ride experience by just giving a thumbs up or a thumbs down.
Icons/Visuals work well, but do they for everyone? Specially when you when want to communicate attitude, friendliness etc.

We changed the design by not including icons. We gave preference to getting a more constructive feedback than saving riders a few seconds. That was our core. (a little trade-offs-in-design lesson for us)


USABILITY TESTING

We went out to the local mall to test our design, and also talked to students who had used UBER to test our design. During our tests, we got a comment by one of the users which made a really good addition to our design. We added an option to 'opt out of a driver', using which riders can avoid having a ride again with a driver they had a very bad experience with.








What I learned

I learned how to effectively use storyboarding to present a design. Also, my story should be able help me as a designer to empathize with the user in the story.
I learned to embrace constraints and identify trade offs.
That we should design, fail fast and make it better by iterating.
