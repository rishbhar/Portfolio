Prehistoric Archaeology
The prehistoric archaeological record sheds light on the evolution of human cognitive abilities. The archaeological record has yielded important information regarding technological patterns, foresight and planning, skill, cognitive capabilities, and dexterity. It is appreciated that at any stage of human evolution, we can only be sure that we are seeing minimal expressions of cognitive abilities in the form of the material culture and behavioral patterns of prehistoric hominins. Material culture, nonetheless, is the most important and reliable source of information pertaining to cognitive evolution that we can recover in the archaeological record. We can search for the most complex and exceptional forms of material culture at different stages of human evolution to see evidence for the most advanced level of cognitive abilities and skill manifested at that time.

Before 2.6 million years ago (Ma), there are only two sites that provide tentative evidence of hominin technological behaviors. Modified stones were discovered at Lomekwi in Kenya that date to 3.3 Ma. It should also be noted that a claim has been made for stone tool use and bone modification at approximately 3.4 million years at the site of Dikika, Ethiopia, where two surface mammal bones allegedly have chop-marks and cut-marks made from stone tools. Based on our knowledge of chimpanzee material culture and cultural traits, we can speculate on a range of possible types of tool-use and other types of cultural phenomena that occurred prior to 3.4 Ma, but without hard prehistoric evidence, it still remains a matter of conjecture.

By 2.6 Ma, there is clear evidence of hominin material culture. The archaeological sites from this time are all on the African continent. The archaeological record is characterized by a simple Oldowan technology, normally made with river cobbles that were knapped with hammerstones to produce sharp flakes and fragments. Even in this early period, there seems to be some indication of selection for higher-quality raw materials, some transport of stone from their sources, and processing of meat and marrow/brains with stone tools. By 1.75 Ma, the early Acheulian industry appears, which includes crude handaxes, cleavers, and picks made on large flakes struck from boulder-cores or made on large cobbles. Around the same time, hominins began to move out of Africa and disperse into Eurasia; evidence of this can be seen at sites such as Dmanisi in the Republic of Georgia and Sangiran in Indonesia. Handaxes and cleavers became better-made, more symmetrical, and more extensively flaked over time. By this technological stage, there are recurrences of very similar forms that suggest the emergence of style and a concept of a “mental template,” or the idea of a distinct artifact form in the mind of the toolmaker. Such templates become even more standardized in the subsequent later Acheulean, Middle Palaeolithic, and Upper Palaeolithic.


Between 750,000 and 250,000 years ago, a number of technological developments associated with Homo heidelbergensis are found around the Old World. These include the development of refined Later Acheulian tools, which commonly include extremely symmetrical and finely fashioned handaxes and cleavers and often show very intricate, controlled flaking, the use of careful platform preparation and of soft hammer percussion in their production. Additional advances observed in the archaeological record include apparent evidence of some controlled use of fire, though its incidence was rare and may not have involved skilled production of fire, as well as the possible emergence of early ritual or symbolic behavior.

Changes associated with Neandertals in Eurasia add more complex (hafted) tools, apparent habitual use of fire, use of personal adornment, and burial of the dead to the adaptive and behavioral repertoire. Such trends continued in earnest among the modern human populations as they grew and spread, eventually involving the addition of other bone, antler, and ivory materials to the toolmaking systems, the appearance of needles and sewing, the development of habitual architecture in various forms, and evidence for long-distance transport and trade of materials. The emergence and evolution of complex symbolic behavior more recently in the archaeological record is evidenced in highly endowed burials, often prolific use of ornaments, and the proliferation of artistic traditions, including sometimes elaborate decoration of utilitarian tools. The emergence of regional patterning and the evidence for complex symbolic behaviors may indicate evolution of complex language systems and abilities during this later stage of human evolution.

Dikika
LOCATION : Afar, Ethiopia DATED TO : 3.42 - 3.24 Ma
Major Discovery
Hominins prior to 2.6 Ma left behind relatively few traces of their cognition and behavior for us to discover in the archaeological record. Possibly the earliest evidence for stone tool use by hominins comes from the site of Dikika in Ethiopia, where a team led by Zeray Alemseged of the California Academy of Sciences in San Francisco discovered two 3.4 million-year-old fossilized animal bones that bear potential cut marks caused by stone tools. This finding could indicate that Australopithecus afarensis butchered large mammals with the assistance of stone tools.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition:
Because Oldowan toolmakers by 2.6 Ma already demonstrate a high level of skill, several researchers have suggested that a simpler stone tool must have existed prior to the Oldowan industry. If the marks on the Dikika bones are cut marks, then they may have been caused by unmodified stone tools, which would resemble the stone tool use behaviors of modern apes. Australopithecus afarensis was thus likely ape-like in cognition and behavior.

Lomekwi
LOCATION : West Turkana, Kenya DATED TO : 3.3 Ma
Major Discovery
Stone tools dating back to 3.3 Ma were discovered at Lomekwi by a team led by Sonia Harmand of Stony Brook University. If this date turns out to be correct, then that would mean that these are the oldest stone tools yet known, and it would push the date of chipped stone technologies back by 700,000 years, before the appearance of the genus Homo.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
The tools at Lomekwi correspond in time with the small-brained Australopithecus afarensis species, which represents a paradigm shift in paleoanthropology. Scientists have long considered members of the genus Homo to be the first stone toolmakers, but now we have to consider that smaller-brained hominins may have possessed the motor coordination, attention, and inventiveness to make stone tools as well. The sharp flakes found at Lomekwi were likely made by bipolar and passive hammering methods rather than striking a hammer stone against a core (flintknapping) like we see in later industries. These reduction methods presumably involve less skill and planning than flintknapping.

Gona
LOCATION : Afar, Ethiopia DATED TO : 2.6 Ma
Major Discovery
When first reported by Sileshi Semaw and his team in 1997, the 2.6 million-year-old artifacts found at the site of Gona were thought to be the oldest chipped stone tools in the world. With the discovery of 3.3 million-year-old tools at the site of Lomekwi, Gona may now only represent the earliest evidence of the Oldowan industry.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Archaeologists are still in disagreement about the extent to which Oldowan cognition differs from non-human great ape cognition. Some researchers have suggested that the cognition required to make Oldowan tools surpasses that possessed by great apes because even the earliest Oldowan tools at sites like Gona, Omo, and Lokalalei 2C demonstrate a level of planning, flexibility, and problem-solving that is not seen in the tool use behaviors of our close ape relatives. Other researchers claim that many of the motor and cognitive elements usually attributed to Oldowan toolmaking, such as precision handling, accurately aimed and powerful blows, bimanual role differentiation, imitation, sequential planning, hierarchical organization, and manual lateralization, can also be found among the great apes.

Kanjera South
LOCATION : Kenya DATED TO : 2 Ma
Major Discovery
Numerous, relatively complete carcasses of small hoofed animals were found at Kanjera South, where they had been butchered with Oldowan stone tools. These tools were made from raw materials that were transported over 10 km. This site represents the earliest evidence for persistent carnivory by hominins in the archaeological record.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Humans are not the only great apes to eat meat, but humans are the only great apes that persistently consume the flesh of other animals. Regular meat consumption is thought to be a leading reason for why the human brain was able to expand to such a large size over time. Primates that rely on complex ways of obtaining food tend to require enhanced cognitive skills, and this would have been especially the case for early humans as they exploited a wide variety of food items on the grasslands. When we find evidence of meat consumption in the early archaeological record, this also brings up the question of whether hominins were acquiring the meat by scavenging or by hunting. It is usually assumed by researchers that hunting requires increased cooperation among individuals relative to scavenging. The evidence at Kanjera South is suggestive of cooperative hunting, which may mean that this population of early Homo had enhanced social cognition skills, such as imitation, gaze following, and perhaps an enhanced communication system. The long distance transport of rocks to make tools also indicates foresight and planning that surpasses that of modern apes.

Dmanisi
LOCATION : Republic of Georgia DATED TO : 1.85-1.78 Ma
Major Discovery
Dmanisi represents the earliest known migration of early humans outside of Africa. The hominins at this site were small in stature and brain size and made Oldowan stone tools.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Homo erectus is the first species of hominin to migrate out of Africa. Modern human-like body proportions, hunting behavior, and long-distance travel for raw materials--these are all indicators of a new adaptive strategy by H. erectus, one that required large home ranges. This new adaptive strategy may have selected for enhanced spatial cognition and long-term memory.

Kokiselei 4
LOCATION : West Turkana, Kenya DATED TO : 1.76 Ma
Major Discovery
After more than 800,000 years of making simple Oldowan flakes, there was a shift toward making large cutting tools and bifacial handaxes, which are characteristic of the Acheulian industry. The earliest dated Acheulian stone tools were discovered by Christopher Lepre (Rutgers University) and his team at the site of Kokiselei 4 and date to 1.76 Ma. Other early occurrences of the Acheulian industry have also been found at Konso, Ethiopia, dated to 1.75 Ma, and FLK West, Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania, dated to 1.7 Ma. This early appearance of Acheulian tools coincides with an increase in brain size in early Homo.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
The appearance of the Acheulian industry is thought to mark a significant transition in the evolution of hominin cognition and perhaps language because of the apparent complexity of these tools relative to earlier stone tool industries. For example, some researchers have suggested that the transmission of skills associated with the Acheulian industry from one individual to another would require complex imitation and shared intentionality. The general symmetry seen across handaxes has prompted some scholars to propose that Acheulian hominins were capable of planning into the future and holding mental representations of the intended end goal shape. Working memory and hierarchical cognition may have also been important to Acheulian hominins as they held in mind the multiple subgoals of handaxe manufacture. Other scholars, however, are not convinced that the end shape of handaxes was intentional on the toolmaker’s part, which could call into question some of the cognitive features that have been attributed to H. erectus if this turns out to be a valid critique.

Sangiran
LOCATION : Java, Indonesia DATED TO : 1.6 Ma
Major Discovery
Sangiran is the earliest dated hominin site in Southeast Asia. When H. erectus arrived to this region, they were probably able to walk over land to the now island of Java because of lower sea levels. Changing sea levels over the next million years may have isolated this population from other hominins in Asia. Stone tools from this time period are rare in Java, which means that H. erectus likely relied on perishable materials like shells and bamboo for tools.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
We cannot automatically assume that H. erectus on Java lacked the same cognitive skills as their African counterparts because they did not make Acheulian stone tools. If they were exploiting novel materials like shells and bamboo for tools, this points to a certain cognitive flexibility and inventiveness that allowed them to imagine potential uses for natural materials they may have never before seen. This also illustrates that these early technologies were culturally transmitted, which means that knowledge of how to make a certain tool type could be lost to a population, unless reinvented.

Arbib, M.A. (2011). From mirror neurons to complex imitation in the evolution of language and tool use. Annual Review of Anthropology 40, 257-273.

Sima del Elefante
LOCATION : Atapuerca, Spain DATED TO : 1.2 Ma
Major Discovery
Sima del Elefante is a cave site with the earliest dated hominin fossils and stone artifacts in western Europe. The lithic assemblage is characterized by low technical investment, and the stones were transported over a very short distance.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
The seasonality of western Europe and the large number of carnivorous mammals in the region may have been challenging for hominins. This could explain the low incidence of hominin sites in this region during the early Pleistocene compared to the Middle Pleistocene. Resources in the vicinity of the cave were abundant, however, and fossil evidence indicates that hominins had primary access to prey, either by hunting or scavenging. The hominins at this site probably possessed similar cognitive skills to African and Asian H. erectus.

Wolo Sege
LOCATION : Flores, Indonesia DATED TO : 1.02 Ma
Major Discovery
Stone tools at this site indicate an early occupation of the island of Flores by early Homo. While we know that fossil humans could reach some Indonesian islands, such as Java, by foot because of low sea levels at the time, Flores is a different story. Located east of the Wallace Line, this island could have only been reached by sea crossing. Occupation of this island may therefore represent the earliest example of manmade watercraft.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
If the first hominins on Flores arrived to the island by traveling on manmade watercraft, this would have important implications for their cognitive and linguistic skills. For example, several researchers have argued that building a sea vessel would require future planning, detailed social collaboration, and language. Only tenuous archaeological evidence for symbolism exists this early in time, however, casting some doubt on the idea of manmade watercraft. Other primates, such as orangutans and gibbons, also inhabit Indonesian islands east of the Wallace line, so it could be more likely that, like other primates, hominins arrived at Flores by accident on natural rafts that got swept out to sea.

Wonderwerk Cave
LOCATION : South Africa DATED TO : 1 Ma
Major Discovery
A team of archaeologists returned to Wonderwerk Cave to examine the evidence for Peter Beaumont’s claim for control of fire as early as 1.7 Ma. The hearthlike feature that Beaumont had reported on turned out to be a natural feature and not manmade. The team made an important new discovery, however--evidence for burning of plant and animal remains in a younger layer dated to 1 Ma. These materials were burned within the cave at a temperature of 450-700° C, the temperature of a small campfire. This is the earliest evidence for controlled fire by H. erectus.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
If hominins were controlling fire as early as 1 Ma, this would have several implications for the cognition of H. erectus. The coordination of multiple individuals was likely necessary for the production and maintenance of a fire, which suggests that H. erectus was capable of joint attention, understanding the intentions of others, and enhanced communication. The multiple steps involved in building a fire would also likely require an extended working memory and future planning, as well as the ability to inhibit behaviors in order to keep the fire from going out.

Olorgesailie
LOCATION : Kenya DATED TO : 0.9 Ma
Major Discovery
Olorgesailie is an unusual site because of the thousands of handaxes that have been found there. Pigments older than 340,000 years have also been discovered at the site.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
The large quantity of handaxes at Olorgesailie might indicate that this site held some symbolic or indexical significance to the hominins who frequented it. In other words, its place on the landscape may have had some cultural significance to H. erectus. There have also been a number of hypotheses put forward for the function of these handaxes. In 1981, Eileen O’Brien proposed that the Olorgesailie handaxes were used as projectile weapons. William Calvin expanded on this idea, stating that the symmetry and flatness of handaxes was not aesthetic but rather played a more pragmatic role. He argued that they were shaped for accurate throwing. Hurling handaxes may have produced a strong selection pressure for neurons that helped control timing of actions. This may have paved the way for language to evolve, according to Calvin. Experimental research has demonstrated, however, that replicative handaxes are difficult to throw because of their sharp edges and they endure impact damage when thrown, which we do not see on handaxes from the archaeological record.

Kathu Pan
LOCATION : South Africa DATED TO : 0.55 - 0.50 Ma
Major Discovery
There have been several important discoveries at the site of Kathu Pan. The lithic assemblage at the site is one of the earliest occurrences of blade technology. The hominin toolmakers at this site used hard hammer percussion to strike off blades from extensively prepared cores. Some of these blades were then retouched to make them into points. There are also signs on the stone points that they were hafted to a shaft and functioned as spear tips, making this the earliest evidence for hafted multicomponent tools.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Blade technology has generally been considered a feature of modern human behavior. The process of preparing a core to make blade tools has been likened to the combination of meaningless phonemes to form a meaningful word or statement. Composite tools are multicomponent tools. The process of making a composite tool has been argued to require complex, future planning. The ability to haft a stone point to a shaft utilizes mental rotation. To carry out this operation, the hominins at this site would have needed an enhanced working memory and attention span and needed to be capable of complex reasoning. Some researchers have suggested that making blades and hafting spear points were such complex skills to learn that the transmission of these skills would have required language.

Boxgrove
LOCATION : West Sussex, England DATED TO : 0.5 Ma
Major Discovery
Boxgrove is a Lower Paleolithic site in England that consists of refined Acheulian handaxe tools and butchered animal bones, all of which were found in situ, or in an undisturbed context. The emphasis of the hominins at the site seemed to be on handaxes rather than flakes, as the flakes show no signs of retouch and remain where they fell during handaxe production. The handaxes were reduced almost completely with a soft hammer, such as an antler, rather than with a hard hammer, and the toolmakers prepared platforms on the bifaces to more effectively control the size and shape of the flakes they removed.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
There has been some debate over whether handaxes were highly-designed, intended tools or the unintentional result of creating large flakes. The handaxes at Boxgrove support the hypothesis that the shape and form of the biface were intentional on the part of the toolmaker. Symmetry and overall aesthetics of the handaxe were important to the toolmaker, while the flakes were unused and discarded. As intended tools, the level of skill required to make the Boxgrove handaxes would have had specific cognitive demands, including hierarchical behavior organization and enhanced planning. Because of the difficulty of making refined handaxes, it is possible that this skill would have required complex imitation, joint attention, and pedagogy for successful transmission of the skill.

Trinil
LOCATION : Java, Indonesia DATED TO : 0.5 Ma
Major Discovery
In a fossil freshwater shell assemblage originally collected by Eugène Dubois in 1891, a team of researchers rediscovered a shell with a geometric design engraved into it. The sediment within the shells date the assemblage to 500,000 years ago, which means that the abstract design was likely made by H. erectus. This is currently the earliest evidence for abstract engraving in the archaeological record.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
It is usually assumed that modern cognition first originated in Africa, but the discovery of an engraved shell in Indonesia indicates that the story of human cognitive evolution is more complicated than this. The shellfish at this site were a meat source for H. erectus. Their shells were also used as tools and apparently as a canvas for abstract expression. The ability to imagine multiple functions for the same item reflects cognitive flexibility. There is some evidence for cognitive flexibility prior to 500 Ka with the appearance of multipurpose Acheulian handaxes in Africa, and this Trinil shell assemblage is likely an extension of this cognitive skill. Purposeful etching of a geometric design into a shell implies that H. erectus was capable of fine motor control of the hands, abstract thinking, and possibly symbolism, key components of language and modern cognition.

Sima de los huesos
LOCATION : Atapuerca, Spain DATED TO : 0.43 Ma
Major Discovery
The fossil bones of at least 28 archaic Neandertals were discovered in a pit of the Cueva Mayor-Cueva del Silo cave system of the Sierra de Atapuerca, along with one quartzite handaxe with no accompanying flake debris. Some researchers have suggested that the bodies of these individuals were purposely thrown down the 13 m shaft post-mortem by other members of their group. This has been a controversial claim, however. Other researchers argue that the presence of other animal remains in the pit in addition to human remains means that animals and hominins alike fell into the pit by accident and then could not get back out. The hominin fossils were all found in the same stratigraphic layer, while other animal remains continued to be deposited after this event. If the hominins at this site were intentionally thrown into the pit, then this would be the earliest evidence of human burial.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
If the hominins at this site were intentionally thrown into the pit, this would not automatically mean that the act was symbolic or that the group they belonged to believed in an afterlife. There is very little evidence for symbolic thought this early in time, and the first true burials did not occur for another 200,000 years. Disposal of dead bodies in the pit may have been for practical reasons, such as keeping large carnivores away from camp, for example. Even if the reason behind these individuals’ interment was not symbolic, there are still some important implications to be made about hominin cognition at this time. The deliberate disposal of their dead implies that these hominins were capable of problem-solving and planning. They also had a good understanding of their place within their environment and how to manipulate the environment to a certain extent.

Terra Amata
LOCATION : Nice, France DATED TO : 0.4 Ma
Major Discovery
In 1966, archaeologist Henry de Lumley discovered a habitation site in Nice where archaic humans lived along a prehistoric beach around 400,000 years ago. Here they made Acheulian stone tools and constructed the earliest dated, open-air living structures. de Lumley believed that the huts were probably made out of animal hides on poles, held up by low walls of stone. Within each structure he found ash that suggests early domestication of fire. These claims have been critiqued by Paola Villa, who argued that the position of the stones at the site was caused by natural processes.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
The structures at Terra Amata were large enough to shelter many individuals, and the hearth would have formed the center of attention and social activity. A behavior as complex as building large structures out of multiple materials would have required a high degree of cooperation, problem-solving, and planning.

Campitello
LOCATION : Italy DATED TO : 0.2 Ma
Major Discovery
The process of hafting a point onto a shaft was likely developed independently several times in prehistory. At Campitello, however, Neandertals developed the first known synthetic material, an adhesive called pitch, which they made by heating birch bark in an oxygen-free environment at around 650° F for several hours. This adhesive improved the durability of their hafted tools and allowed for a stronger bond between the point and the shaft.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
The process of making pitch would have required Neandertals to have an in-depth understanding of pyrotechnology, enhanced working memory, the ability to conceptualize the past and future, flexibly switch between tasks, as well as innovate. Neandertals also would have needed a complex communication system to coordinate this activity. Their ability to make pitch as one component of a composite tool is currently the best evidence we have that Neandertals’ cognition rivaled that of early modern humans evolving in Africa.

Pinnacle Point
LOCATION : South Africa DATED TO : 0.164 Ma
Major Discovery
Heat treating certain types of stone, such as silcrete, improves the stone’s flaking abilities. Heat treating stone involves placing unmodified stone into a very hot fire. With increased hardness, these stones are more likely to fracture in the way a toolmaker intends, instead of breaking along internal imperfections in the structure of the stone. This practice became widespread by 72,000 years ago, but the first evidence of this behavior is found at Pinnacle Point 164,000 years ago.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Some researchers have suggested that heat-treating stone for its improved flaking properties signals complex cognition. These early humans would have required sophisticated knowledge of pyrotechnology and the principles of applying heat to different materials. Indeed, heat treating flint is a complicated and time-consuming process that requires the creation and maintenance of precise conditions, but the material found at Pinnacle Point is silcrete. The process of heat treating silcrete may not have been as involved as heat treating flint. It could have been easily performed on fires used for cooking or other activities.

Es-Skhul Cave
LOCATION : Israel DATED TO : 0.135 - 0.1 Ma
Major Discovery
The rock shelter site known as Skhul was excavated in the 1930s. More recently, a research team discovered shells in the collections that had been recovered from the site. Skhul lies 3.5 km away from the Mediterranean seashore, which means that these shells were purposely selected and transported to the cave by premodern humans. Moreover, they have human-made perforations, and the edges of these perforations are rounded, indicating that the shells were worn on a string as beads. They were also stained with ochre. These shells currently represent the earliest evidence for personal ornamentation using beads.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Many researchers agree that hominins were more than likely capable of symbolic behavior if they were wearing beads as ornamentation. In this case, their bodies became a medium for communication. This implies that these hominins had a self-awareness akin to that of modern humans. Their cognition was evolving along with changing social and demographic conditions in an ever more complex social landscape.

Krapina
LOCATION : Croatia DATED TO : 0.13 Ma
Major Discovery
Symbolic behavior like personal ornamentation through the use of jewelry has long been considered by researchers as a hallmark of modern human cognition. However, this site shows that personal ornamentation may have been independently developed by Neanderthals earlier than previously thought. Researchers have identified cut marks and wear marks on several eagle talons. The wear marks on the talons are consistent with the expected wear marks that would develop if they were strung together on a bracelet or necklace. Krapina also has the distinction of being one of the oldest, undisputed burial sites in the world.
Read more about this discovery
How it relates to the evolution of cognition
Symbolic items are rarely discovered at Neandertal sites. When modern human-like behaviors are associated with Neandertal remains, it is common for researchers to argue that Neandertals came into contact with Homo sapiens and imitated their behaviors. The early dates at Krapina exclude this possibility, however, which means that Neandertals were making jewelry long before modern humans arrived in Europe. If they were catching the eagles to make the talon pendants, this would have required planning. And if they wore the talons as jewelry, this implies that they had a well-developed theory of mind, a sense of self-awareness, and possibly a symbolic communication system.

The shell bead industry demonstrates that they were likely capable of self-awareness and theory of mind. Ornamentation such as this may have contributed to group identity.
